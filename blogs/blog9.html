Empowering Women Through the Internet in Africa

25 April 2024 by Aaminah Habib

Across the globe, the drive towards digital equality is gaining momentum, yet in Africa, women's access to this digital revolution remains staggeringly limited. The underrepresentation of African women in the digital space not only mirrors but also magnifies the gender inequities prevalent across the continent. This blog goes into the insights provided by Chenai Chair Magenya, who illuminates the path toward not just bridging this digital divide but also using technology as a catalyst for gender empowerment and systemic change. As we explore Chenai Chair Magenya’s insights, it becomes clear that digital technology offers more than just connectivity; it offers a pathway to empowerment and equality. As Magenya puts it in her analysis, 'A feminist internet works to bridge the divide between gender inequalities.' Magenya’s perspective not only highlights disparities but also calls for a transformative approach that ensures women can use the internet as a tool for real empowerment.

While the digital landscape in Africa presents numerous barriers to women's full participation, strategic interventions and community-focused initiatives can pave the way for an inclusive digital future where every woman has the tools to thrive.

Insights from Magenya:

Magenya introduces her argument by framing the internet not just as a technological tool but as a space fraught with gender disparities. Her thesis posits that a feminist approach to internet governance and accessibility can significantly impact gender equity in Africa. This sets the stage for examining the socio-political contexts that influence these disparities.

Magenya uses terms each carrying significant weight in discussing internet usage. The choice of these words is intended to provoke thought about inclusivity and the broader implications of being digitally connected or disconnected. Some uses of key terms are Digital Divide. This term is crucial as it encapsulates the gap between those who have access to modern information and communication technology and those who do not.

By using this term, Magenya highlights not just a disparity in access but a resultant gap in power, opportunities, and outcomes. It sets the stage for discussing how this divide specifically affects women and deepens existing societal inequalities. Frequently used in gender-related discourse, "empowerment" in Magenya’s text refers to giving women the tools, capabilities, and access necessary to overcome societal and economic barriers. It implies an active dismantling of obstacles and the enhancement of agency among African women through digital means. Using” Accessibility”, Magenya touches on the availability and usability of digital technologies. Accessibility is not just about the physical availability of the internet but also about how accessible it is in terms of cost, usability, language, and relevance to women’s lives in diverse African contexts.

Magenya’s use of metaphors and analogies to bridge the abstract concepts of internet connectivity with the tangible realities of daily life.“Internet as a Gateway” , metaphor suggests that the internet opens doors to opportunities previously out of reach, particularly for women. It can lead to educational resources, global marketplaces, and platforms for political and social expression. The gateway metaphor embodies potential and new beginnings, framing the internet as a critical tool for development and personal growth.“Internet as a Barrier”, Conversely, when the internet acts as a barrier, it becomes a divider or wall that excludes those who cannot access it from these opportunities. This perspective highlights how lack of access solidifies existing disparities. The barrier metaphor effectively illustrates how without intervention, technology can exacerbate social inequities instead of alleviating them.

By merging these terms and metaphors, Magenya constructs a nuanced narrative around the internet's dual potential to empower and to marginalize. Her language choices encapsulate the critical stakes involved: either the internet becomes a transformative tool for feminist advancement in Africa or continues to reinforce traditional gender hierarchies.

Magenya effectively structures her argument. Magenya begins by situating the issue within the global framework of digital access, discussing how disparities in internet access are a worldwide issue that disproportionately affects marginalized groups, particularly women. This sets the stage for a deeper investigation into how these global patterns manifest specifically in Africa. After establishing a global backdrop, Magenya narrows her focus to Africa. She discusses specific barriers African women face, such as socio-economic constraints, cultural norms, and inadequate policy frameworks that hinder their full participation in the digital world. This shift from the global to the regional allows her to dive into detailed examples that illustrate the broader themes introduced earlier. Further structuring her argument, Magenya integrates case studies and examples from various African countries. These serve to concretize her points, offering tangible instances of both the challenges and successes in bridging the digital gender gap. This method not only enriches her narrative but also provides a clearer picture of the practical applications and implications of her arguments. Concluding her analysis, Magenya discusses potential solutions and strategies that could address these disparities. This section includes policy recommendations, community-based initiatives, and technological solutions tailored to the African context, thus providing a hopeful outlook on future possibilities.

Below I will unpack my thoughts and research based off the insights from Magenya.

Gender-Specific Barriers to Internet Access

Women often face economic disadvantages that directly impact their ability to access digital technologies. Globally, women tend to earn less than men, a disparity that extends to most countries regardless of economic status. This wage gap means that women have less disposable income to spend on non-essentials, including technology and data plans. In many regions, the cost of a mobile device—a primary tool for internet access—is prohibitively high for those earning lower incomes. For women, who are statistically more likely to be in lower-wage jobs or informal employment, acquiring such technology can be particularly challenging. The cost of internet data remains high in many parts of the world. For women with limited financial resources, these costs can be a significant barrier to regular internet use. According to the Alliance for Affordable Internet , high data prices disproportionately affect women, especially in developing countries where 1GB of data can exceed 5% of a woman's monthly income.

Cultural factors can also restrict women's access to the internet. In many cultures, there is a prevailing belief that technology is more necessary or appropriate for men, who are often seen as the primary beneficiaries of education and employment opportunities. This bias can influence household spending priorities, leading to men and boys receiving greater access to digital devices and connectivity. In some societies, women's mobility is restricted compared to men's, limiting their physical access to public internet facilities such as cafes and libraries. Social norms may also discourage women from engaging in communities and networks that facilitate digital literacy and empowerment.

Lack of supportive policies and infrastructure exacerbates these disparities. Women are often underrepresented in the sectors that decide internet governance and infrastructure development, leading to policies that overlook or fail to address the specific needs of women. Many infrastructure development policies focus on urban and commercially lucrative areas, often neglecting rural and underserved regions where many women reside.

Women that do have/get internet access also face dark struggles. women are not only more likely to be victims of cyber harassment, but the nature of this harassment is often sexualized or gender-specific, which can have severe psychological effects. Studies such as the one by Amnesty International have shown that women are disproportionately targeted by gender-based violence online, including threats of physical violence and sexual harassment. The fear induced by potential or actual online harassment can lead women to self-censor, limit their online presence, or abandon empowering digital platforms altogether. This reduction in participation can directly affect their career opportunities, educational resources, and social networks.

Women’s health data, location data, and even financial information can become areas of vulnerability when shared online. The misuse of this data can lead to manipulative marketing practices, predatory loans, or worse, physical safety risks. Research by the Privacy Rights Clearinghouse highlights specific cases where women's data has been misused, leading to real-world consequences such as stalking and public shaming, which underscore the need for better protective measures in digital spaces.

Some argue that digital literacy alone does not guarantee empowerment. Critics argue that while digital literacy provides necessary skills, it often results in superficial engagement with technology that doesn't translate into substantial life improvements. They point out that having the skills to navigate the internet or use digital tools doesn’t necessarily mean that women can leverage these skills for economic or social gains, particularly if the structural barriers at play aren't addressed simultaneously. Another concern is that digital literacy initiatives might actually reinforce existing inequalities. If such programs are more accessible to women in urban areas or those already somewhat empowered, the gap widens between them and their rural or less privileged counterparts. This can create a new elite class that benefits from digital advancements while leaving behind the most marginalized, who perhaps need these skills the most. Even with digital skills, economic and cultural barriers can severely limit their application. For instance, women may learn to use digital tools but still face discrimination in the workplace or lack access to entrepreneurial resources, making it difficult to translate digital skills into economic power.

Rwanda has positioned itself as a leader in information and communication technology (ICT) within Africa, implementing several initiatives aimed at transforming the country into a knowledge-based economy. The Rwandan government, in partnership with various NGOs and international organizations, has been promoting the involvement of women in ICT. Programs such as “Girls in ICT Rwanda” have been pivotal. This initiative hosts events, workshops, and competitions that encourage young girls to embark on careers in technology and digital fields. Traditionally, Rwandan society, like many others, had stereotyped roles for women that did not include careers in technology. However, through consistent exposure to ICT skills and seeing women excel in this field, perceptions are changing. Young girls and even older community members are beginning to see technology as a viable and respectable career path for women, challenging previous cultural norms that relegated women to more domestic roles.

The presence of female role models in ICT and the success stories emerging from these programs have not only inspired more girls to join the tech field but have also influenced broader societal attitudes towards women in professions previously dominated by men.

In Kenya, small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) drive the economy, and increasingly, women are at the forefront of this sector. Platforms like Soko, an e-commerce platform that allows artisans to sell their goods worldwide directly from their smartphones, have targeted female entrepreneurs. Soko’s model has enabled Kenyan women artisans to bypass traditional barriers to market entry like middlemen and physical marketplaces, where cultural norms might restrict their business activities. Soko and similar platforms empower women economically but also challenge the normative expectations of women's roles within both the family and community. As women become breadwinners and business leaders, they gain respect and authority, altering traditional gender dynamics.

The success of these women has led to a notable shift in community perceptions about female entrepreneurship. It provides tangible examples of women contributing significantly to household incomes and community development, thereby fostering a cultural shift towards gender equality in business.

While all the mentioned initiatives has shown promising results, the long-term sustainability of such programs remains uncertain. Challenges such as fluctuating funding, dependence on international donors, and the need for continuous technological updates pose significant risks to the persistence and expansion of these initiatives. Despite the advances made by initiatives like Soko, the deep-seated cultural norms viewing women primarily as caregivers rather than entrepreneurs can undermine the broader acceptance and effectiveness of such platforms. These cultural barriers can dilute the impact of digital tools, sometimes relegating them to supplementary rather than transformational roles in women’s lives. These Digital literacy initiatives, while beneficial, often have a biased reach, predominantly benefiting women in urban settings who have prior access to basic technology. This selective accessibility risks widening the digital divide, reinforcing existing socio-economic disparities rather than alleviating them.While digital platforms offer new opportunities for empowerment, they also expose women to increased risks of cyber harassment and privacy violations. These risks can deter women from fully utilizing digital spaces, thus paradoxically reinforcing the barriers these platforms seek to dismantle.Policies promoting digital inclusivity often lack enforcement and real-world applicability, especially in rural areas. Without robust implementation and monitoring, such policies remain theoretical, failing to address the nuanced needs of diverse women across Africa.

Digital Literacy equals women empowerment

According to the International Finance Corporation (IFC), women with digital skills are not only more likely to gain employment but also to ascend to leadership roles within their sectors. The digital environment lessens some of the physical barriers that women face in the workplace, offering a more level playing field. Furthermore, digital platforms provide women the tools to build professional networks, access mentorships, and lifelong learning opportunities, which are critical for career progression. Programs like TechMoms in South Africa teach mothers digital skills, enabling them to re-enter the workforce in tech-related fields. This program has reported higher employment rates among participants, with many moving into sectors such as IT support, web development, and digital marketing. It's important to evaluate where these initiatives have faced difficulties. TechMoms, aimed at equipping mothers in South Africa with digital skills to facilitate their re-entry into the workforce, particularly in tech-related fields. Some participants found the pace of the courses challenging, especially those balancing training with family responsibilities. This affected their retention and successful completion of the program. Post-training employment did not always align with expectations, either due to the scarcity of tech jobs in participants' local areas or the competitive nature of tech job markets.

The rapid expansion of e-commerce has been a significant enabler for female entrepreneurs. Platforms like Jumia and Konga in Nigeria provide women entrepreneurs with the ability to reach a vast customer base without the need for physical stores, significantly reducing the business startup and maintenance costs. UNCTAD's 2020 report highlights that e-commerce platforms break down the geographical barriers that often-confined women to local markets. These platforms allow women to sell their products and services across borders, accessing markets that were previously out of reach and thus maximizing their earning potential. Successful examples include initiatives like the African E-Commerce Development Project, which collaborates with local and international partners to provide training, funding, and support systems specifically tailored for women in e-commerce. These initiatives help women overcome some of the specific challenges they face, such as securing startup capital and finding marketplaces for their products. However, come challenges were that many participants struggled with the logistics of shipping and handling, which hindered their ability to consistently meet order demands. Some entrepreneurs had limited experience with digital tools necessary for managing an e-commerce business, impacting their operational efficiency.SheTradesan initiative by the International Trade Centre (ITC) connects women entrepreneurs globally to markets through its digital platform, providing tools and training to help women exploit e-commerce opportunities. SheTrades has enabled thousands of women in countries like Kenya and Ghana to access global markets, substantially increasing their revenues and promoting sustainable business practices. Some issues with this were Some women found it challenging to penetrate the market due to high competition in certain product categories. In some regions, women still face significant cultural barriers to engaging in international trade, affecting their participation.

Digital campaigns like Google's #IAmRemarkable have used the power of storytelling and digital reach to challenge and redefine the societal perceptions of women’s capabilities. By highlighting the personal and professional achievements of women across the globe, these campaigns offer alternative narratives that challenge traditional gender roles. They serve as inspirational examples that encourage both women and men to acknowledge and support women's roles in diverse fields. These campaigns also increase the visibility of women in traditionally male-dominated industries, such as STEM, politics, and leadership roles, providing role models for young girls and other women. Platforms like She Leads Africa exemplify how digital tools can be instrumental in bridging the mentorship gap. By facilitating connections with established women leaders, these platforms offer guidance, advice, and support that can be crucial for young women's professional and personal development. These online communities also provide a space for women to network, share experiences, and collaborate on projects or business ventures, which can lead to increased opportunities and empowerment.

In Rwanda, initiatives like ‘Girls in ICT’ are redefining the cultural fabric by encouraging young girls to engage in the tech industry, traditionally dominated by men. This program not only equips girls with essential digital skills but also serves to shift societal perceptions, proving that women can excel in and transform the tech industry. Similarly, in Kenya, platforms like Soko are empowering women artisans to take control of their economic futures, challenging traditional norms that restrict women's economic activities to local markets. These platforms not only open up new markets for these women but also elevate their status within their communities, promoting a more inclusive view of women’s roles in society.

Feminist Theory in the Digital Age

Haraway's notion of the "cyborg" in her seminal essay, "A Cyborg Manifesto," is particularly relevant. She argues that traditional boundaries separating human from machine are breaking down, leading to new forms of identity and possibilities of resistance against traditional power structures. In the digital age, this theory can be seen as empowering for women, allowing them to navigate and redefine boundaries that were once rigid and oppressive.

The concept of intersectionality, although originally articulated byKimberlé Crenshaw, has been extensively developed by feminist theorists like bell hooks. It emphasizes how different axes of identities (race, gender, class) intersect to produce unique experiences of oppression and privilege. Applying this to the digital world, Magenya’s analysis could explore how African women's access to digital technologies is shaped by these intersecting identities, creating a varied landscape of digital empowerment and exclusion.

Third-Wave Feminism is a branch of feminism concerned with personal narratives and localized experiences, recognizing that women's experiences of oppression and empowerment are not universal but diverse and culturally specific. In the context of the digital age, this perspective supports Magenya’s focus on African women's specific experiences with technology, challenging global narratives of digital progress that do not fit all contexts.

These feminist theories stress the importance of viewing digital access and literacy not just as economic tools but as avenues for personal liberation and identity formation. They advocate for a digital space that recognizes and adapts to the diversity of women's experiences, promoting a more inclusive approach to technology development and policy.
