Internet and the Just Society

In the digital age, access to the internet is not just a luxury; it's a necessity for participation in the global economy, education, and democracy. Yet, a significant digital divide persists, separating those with access to information and communication technologies (ICTs) from those without. The Institute for Internet and the Just Society highlights the urgent need for inclusive digital governance and fair artificial intelligence to ensure that the digital future is equitable for all.

The Scope of the Divide

The digital divide is multi-dimensional, affecting various communities differently based on geographic, socio-economic, and demographic factors. Rural areas, low-income families, and developing countries often face the brunt of this divide, lacking the infrastructure and resources needed for reliable internet access. Ethical Considering the implications of the digital divide extend beyond mere access to technology; they encompass the right to participate fully in society. Without access to the digital world, individuals are left behind in education, healthcare, and the job market, exacerbating existing inequalities.

Examples That Call for Justice

These examples stress the urgent need for digital inclusion to encourage equality and opportunity for all segments of society.

  • Rural and Remote Areas:

    Households in rural or remote areas frequently struggle with inadequate broadband infrastructure, making it difficult for many to afford digital devices or internet services. This lack of access hinders students' ability to engage in remote learning and limits job opportunities in the digital economy, deepening social stratification and inequality(Kloza, 2023) .

  • Poor Communities:

    A significant facet of the digital divide is its impact on poor communities. For instance, Pew Research Center findings show a severe contrast in digital access between income levels in the U.S., where individuals from lower-income households often lack basic digital resources like smartphones, broadband, and computers. This scarcity notably affects educational outcomes, manifesting as the"homework gap" where students from these backgrounds struggle to access high-speed internet for remote learning and online assignments(Grcar, 2024) .

  • Indigenous Populations:

    Indigenous populations often face significant hurdles in accessing high-speed internet, impacting their ability to participate in digital education, economic opportunities, and community development. For example, less than 2% of on-reserve households in Canada have access to recommended high-speed internet, limiting these communities' participation in essential digital realms (Grcar, 2024).

  • Older Adults:

    The digital divide also significantly impacts older adults, exacerbating issues of social isolation and accessibility in an increasingly online world. Nearly 22 million American seniors are without wireline broadband at home, highlighting a reality of disconnection and isolation for many in this age group (Grcar, 2024) .

  • Global Disparities:

    The divide between developed and developing countries, often referred to as the Global North and Global South divide, underscores the disparities in internet connectivity. While most of the world's population enjoys internet access, only a fraction of those in the least developed countries can say the same, with many using 2G and 3G networks while global shift towards 5G technology (Grcar, 2024) .

    The terms "Global North" and "Global South" represent a modern, socio-economic, and political categorization of countries that goes beyond mere geographic distinctions. The Global North generally comprises developed countries, including Northern America, Europe, Israel, Japan, South Korea, Australia, and New Zealand. These regions are characterized by higher standards of living, advanced economies, and robust infrastructure. The Global South, in contrast, includes countries primarily in Africa, Latin America, the Caribbean, Asia (excluding Israel, Japan, and South Korea), and Oceania (excluding Australia and New Zealand). These countries often face challenges such as lower incomes, higher poverty rates, dense populations, limited educational opportunities, deficient healthcare systems, and poor infrastructure. The Global South is home to emerging powers like China, India, Indonesia, Brazil, and Pakistan, which are significant in terms of land area and population but still face developmental challenges. The distinction between the Global North and South is based on economic status rather than geographical location, as delineated by the Brandt Line in the 1980s. This conceptual line divides the world into richer and poorer countries, highlighting the divide between them. (Wikipedia, 2021).

    The divide isn't merely about who has access to the internet and who doesn't; it's deeply linked with historical, economic, and social disparities that have long existed between developed and developing countries. The historical context of colonialism and imperialism, where the Global North exploited the resources and labor of what is now known as the Global South, has profound implications for today's digital divide. The economic disparities created by these historical processes have direct consequences on the ability of countries in the Global South to invest in the necessary infrastructure for digital connectivity and education on ICT usage. This background enriches our understanding of why certain regions lag in digital access and literacy, underscoring the need for targeted efforts to bridge the digital divide that goes beyond mere infrastructure development (Environment and Nature, 2022).

    These examples illustrate that the digital divide is not merely a matter of technology access but is deeply intertwined with broader issues of social justice, economic inequality, and global disparity. Addressing this divide requires a concerted effort from governments, private companies, and non-profit organizations to ensure universal access to digital technologies and the internet. This includes investing in infrastructure, providing affordable digital access, enhancing digital literacy, and ensuring inclusive policies and practices that consider the needs of all segments of society.

    The ethical imperative to bridge the digital divide is clear. It is about guaranteeing that everyone can participate fully in the digital age, irrespective of their socio-economic status, geographic location, age, or ethnicity. This calls for a reevaluation of priorities and a commitment to digital inclusion as a cornerstone of a just and equitable society.

Towards Global Connectivity

Technological Innovations

Technological innovations such as satellite internet projects spearheaded by companies like SpaceX's Starlink and OneWeb aim to provide high-speed internet access to remote and underserved regions globally. These ventures have the potential to bypass traditional infrastructural challenges, such as the need for ground-based fiber-optic cables. Similarly, local community networks, often grassroots-driven efforts, empower communities to build and manage their internet service, tailored to local needs and conditions. Besides satellite internet and community networks, consider the emerging role of Low Earth Orbit (LEO) satellites, which promise lower latency and higher speeds compared to traditional satellites (TE Connectivity, n.d.). Companies like Amazon’s Project Kuiper are also entering this space, further expanding the potential for global connectivity. Additionally, the development of solar-powered drones and high-altitude balloons for internet service, as explored by projects like Google's Loon (now defunct but insightful), showcases the diversity of approaches to overcoming connectivity barriers (Kohnstamm, 2023).

Technologies and especially AI is not just a tool but a transformative force that could reshape the digital divide for better or worse. The Institute's focus on promoting ethical AI use highlights the critical need to balance innovation with the priorities of privacy, transparency, and equity. The vast data processing capabilities of AI systems, as seen in instances like the 'Cambridge Analytica' scandal, expose the vulnerabilities of our democracies, making it evident that safeguarding personal privacy and ensuring the integrity of our electoral processes are dominant (Confessore, 2018).

However, achieving global internet connectivity requires more than just technological solutions; it demands policy frameworks that prioritize access as a fundamental human right.

The Institute's focus on Digital Governance and Democracy shows the urgent need for robust digital governance frameworks that can keep pace with technological innovation. Echoing Prof. Luciano Floridi's insight, we are reminded that our challenge no longer lies in digital innovation itself but in mastering the governance of the digital scope. This era of rapid technological advancement needs a shift from reactive to proactive governance, ensuring that digital society's direction, pace, and destination are democratically determined, safeguarding the digital rights and freedoms of all citizens.

Policy Frameworks

Policies must recognize internet access as a fundamental human right, akin to access to clean water and education. This involves regulatory measures that encourage competition among ISPs to lower prices, government-funded programs to provide affordable or free internet access to low-income households, and investments in infrastructure in underserved areas. Countries like Finland and Estonia have led the way by legally recognizing the right to internet access, setting a precedent for others to follow.

Beyond recognizing internet access as a right, we need to consider the importance of data protection and privacy legislation in creating a safe online environment. The European Union’s General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) is an example of policy that not only protects users but also builds trust in internet services. Furthermore, policies encouraging the open-source development of software can raise innovation and accessibility in the digital space. In line with the necessity of robust cybersecurity measures, the Institute for Internet & Just Society supports initiatives like the Paris Call for Trust and Security in Cyberspace. This commitment to establishing trust and security in digital interactions mirrors our advocacy for a safer internet, where users' rights and privacy are vital.

International Cooperation

The Institute for Internet and the Just Society's endorsement of the Paris Call for Trust and Security in Cyberspace shows the critical intersection where efforts to establish a secure and trustworthy digital environment converge with initiatives aimed at achieving global internet connectivity.

Achieving global internet connectivity also requires robust international cooperation. Initiatives like the Alliance for Affordable Internet (A4AI) and partnerships between countries, NGOs, and private entities can use collective resources and expertise to tackle the digital divide on a global scale. South-South cooperation, emphasizing mutual support among countries in the Global South, can help in sharing knowledge and technologies that address common challenges.

Understanding the Global North and South divide can inform policy decisions and global cooperation efforts aimed at addressing the digital divide. It suggests that policies must account for the historical and ongoing economic imbalances between countries. Initiatives like South-South cooperation, which encourages collaboration among countries in the Global South, can be a model for similar collaborative efforts in digital inclusion projects. Moreover, it underscores the importance of the Global North's support, not as a savior, but as a partner in rectifying historical wrongs through fair trade, aid, and knowledge exchange that respects the autonomy and development goals of countries in the Global South.

The role of international organizations like the ITU (International Telecommunication Union) and the World Bank in supporting infrastructure projects in developing countries should be highlighted. The ITU helps in coordinating global telecom networks and services. It aids in setting international standards that ensure the interconnection and interoperability of communication systems worldwide, which is crucial for global internet connectivity. The ITU also organizes the allocation of global radio spectrum and satellite orbits, essential resources for mobile and satellite communications. Through its development sector (ITU-D), the ITU implements projects directly aimed at enhancing digital infrastructure in developing countries, focusing on areas such as cybersecurity, broadband deployment, and capacity building (ITU, 2019) .

The World Bank contributes to global connectivity by financing, advising, and researching information and communication technology (ICT) projects in developing countries. These projects often focus on expanding access to broadband internet, improving ICT regulatory frameworks, and fostering digital skills among the populace. The World Bank's engagement helps bridge the financial and knowledge gaps that many developing countries face in building modern digital infrastructure (World Bank, n.d.).

Specifically, SDG 9 focuses on building resilient infrastructure, promoting inclusive and sustainable industrialization, and fostering innovation, with a particular emphasis on significantly increasing access to information and communication technology. By advocating for investments in ICT infrastructure, the SDGs highlight the critical need for high-speed internet as a driver of economic growth, social inclusion, and environmental sustainability. The commitment to SDG 9 by countries worldwide demonstrates a collective acknowledgment of the importance of connectivity in achieving broader development goals (United Nations, 2023).

These organizations and agreements exemplify the global commitment to eliminating the digital divide. By pooling resources, knowledge, and political will, they support infrastructure projects that lay the groundwork for a more connected and equitable world. The collaborative nature of these efforts ensures that initiatives are aligned with the specific needs and circumstances of each country, maximizing the impact of investments in digital infrastructure.

Socio-Economic Considerations

  • Affordability of Internet Services and Hidden Costs

    The cost of internet services remains a significant barrier to digital inclusion. In many regions, especially in developing countries, the cost of data plans and the required hardware (smartphones, computers) can be prohibitively expensive relative to average incomes. Furthermore, the "hidden costs" of digital participation, such as the need for regular device maintenance and updates, add additional layers of financial burden. Governments and organizations must implement strategies to make internet access and digital devices more affordable, through subsidies, public-private partnerships, and support for low-cost technologies.

  • Digital Public Libraries

    Digital public libraries offer a pivotal resource in bridging the digital divide. By providing free access to digital resources and internet services, these institutions support educational and economic opportunities for poor populations. They serve as community hubs for digital literacy training, helping users navigate and utilize digital tools effectively. The American Library Association and other similar entities globally advocate for the expansion of digital services in libraries to promote community resilience and empowerment (American Library Association, 2023).

  • Mobile Money Services

    The impact of mobile money services on financial inclusion cannot be overstated. In regions like East Africa, services such as M-Pesa have revolutionized how individuals and businesses conduct transactions, offering an accessible platform for those previously excluded from the formal banking system(GSMA, 2017) . The widespread adoption of mobile money has not only facilitated easier, safer, and more efficient transactions but has also spurred economic growth and entrepreneurship. It demonstrates the potential of accessible digital tools to transform economic landscapes, making a compelling case for the role of technology in achieving broader socio-economic inclusion (ITU, 2017).

    Inclusivity should be at the heart of all efforts to expand internet connectivity. This means designing services and content that are accessible to people with disabilities, supporting content in multiple languages to cater to diverse populations, and ensuring that online spaces are safe and welcoming for all users. The principles of Design Justice can guide these efforts, ensuring that marginalized communities have a voice in shaping the digital world.

Designing for Inclusion

These are some ways for creating technologies and policies that do not just bridge the digital divide but actively dismantle the structures that perpetuate it. By centering the voices and needs of marginalized communities in the design process, we can build a more equitable digital future.

Looking Forward

As we explore the complexities of the digital divide and the multifaceted approaches required to address it, the mission of the Institute for Internet and the Just Society serves as a guiding light for actionable change. The Institute emphasizes the necessity of inclusive digital governance and equitable AI, principles that resonate deeply with the solutions and challenges discussed in this blog.

The Institute's work in advocating for policies that ensure internet access as a fundamental human right aligns with our exploration of supportive policy frameworks. Their initiatives towards creating a fairer digital landscape offer practical steps that governments, organizations, and individuals can take to make the digital future accessible to all. By supporting efforts like the Alliance for Affordable Internet and emphasizing the importance of international cooperation, the Institute mirrors the global call to action for digital inclusivity.

Also, the Institute's dedication to overcoming socio-economic barriers through digital literacy programs and infrastructure development provides a way for addressing the challenges faced by various communities worldwide. Their focus on the Global North and South divide offers insights into the historical and economic contexts that intensify the digital divide, urging a comprehensive approach that considers the legacy of inequality and the path towards a more equitable distribution of digital resources.

The work of the Institute for Internet and the Just Society and all that I’ve shared above have a shared vision for a world where digital technologies serve as a foundation for empowerment, education, and equality. This Institute's ongoing projects and research offer valuable resources and frameworks for anyone committed to closing the digital divide.

As readers and digital citizens, we are called to support and engage with initiatives like those by the Institute for Internet and the Just Society. Whether through advocacy, volunteering, or simply by staying informed, we can all contribute to the collective effort to ensure that the digital age benefits everyone, regardless of socio-economic status, geographic location, age, or ethnicity.
